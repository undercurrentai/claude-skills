# Legal Writing Voice Guide

This skill defines a legal writing voice derived from the collected authority of Bryan
Garner, Ross Guberman, Joseph Kimble, Richard Wydick, Justice Scalia, Kenneth Adams,
and 30+ additional legal writing sources. The voice produces text that reads like it was
drafted by an experienced, disciplined attorney: clear, precise, authoritative, and free
of AI tells.

The guiding principle: write for the reader, not for yourself. Every word must earn its
place. Legal writing exists to communicate, persuade, and create binding obligations. It
does not exist to showcase vocabulary, signal erudition, or fill pages.

"Good writers make complex ideas simple; bad writers make simple ideas complex." — Garner

## CRITICAL: Zero Tolerance for LLM-isms

Legal writing demands credibility above all else. AI-generated language patterns destroy
credibility instantly. Before producing ANY text using this skill, scan the output for
every item in the ban lists below and replace or remove them. This is the single most
important rule in this skill. If the output reads like it could have been generated by a
chatbot, it has failed.

The ban lists below are not exhaustive. The general principle: if a word or phrase feels
like something a corporate chatbot or a first-year associate padding a brief would write,
do not use it.

### Banned words

NEVER use these words in legal writing produced by this skill. They are the most
statistically overrepresented words in LLM-generated text, backed by peer-reviewed
research (Kobak et al. 2025, Liang et al. 2024):

**Adjectives:** delve/delving, actionable, bespoke, bustling, captivating, commendable,
comprehensive (as filler), cutting-edge, daunting, ever-evolving, game-changing,
groundbreaking, holistic, impactful, innovative, insightful, intricate, invaluable,
meticulous, multifaceted, notable, noteworthy, nuanced, paramount, pivotal, profound,
remarkable, revolutionary, robust (outside technical contexts), seamless,
state-of-the-art, substantial (as filler), tailored, thought-provoking, transformative,
unparalleled, unwavering, vibrant, whimsical

**Nouns:** beacon, blueprint (metaphorical), ecosystem (metaphorical), enigma, frontier,
implications (vague), interplay, intricacies, journey (metaphorical), kaleidoscope,
landscape (metaphorical), linchpin, metropolis, milestone (metaphorical), paradigm,
paradigm shift, plethora, quest, realm, resonance, roadmap (metaphorical), synergy,
tapestry, testament, touchpoint, treasure trove, value proposition

**Verbs:** align (metaphorical), amplify, bolster, champion, craft (as "craft a
solution"), delve, elevate, embark, empower, encompass, enhance, enrich, facilitate,
foster, harness, leverage, navigate (metaphorical), pioneer, resonate, safeguard,
shed light, showcase, spearhead, streamline (outside technical contexts), supercharge,
tailor, transcend, underscore, uncover, unleash, unlock, unpack, unravel, unveil,
utilize (just say "use")

**Adverbs:** accordingly, arguably, consequently, effectively (filler), essentially
(filler), furthermore, hence, holistically, meticulously, moreover, namely, nonetheless,
notwithstanding (as transition — acceptable as a legal term in specific contract
provisions), seamlessly, significantly (filler), strategically, subsequently,
substantially (filler), thereby, therefore (overused — use sparingly), thus (overused),
ultimately (overused), undeniably, undoubtedly

### Legal-specific banned words and phrases

These are the tells of lazy legal writing. They signal either AI generation or an
undisciplined drafter:

**Credibility-destroying qualifiers — NEVER use as filler:**
- "clearly" / "clearly established" — if it were clear, you would not need to say so.
  Reserve only for the legal standard "clearly established" in qualified immunity analysis
  where the term is doctrinal.
- "obviously" / "it is obvious that" — same problem. Weak arguments hide behind this word.
- "undoubtedly" / "without question" / "beyond dispute" — let the analysis speak.
- "egregious" — almost always hyperbole. Use only when describing conduct that genuinely
  shocks the conscience, and even then sparingly.

**Throat-clearing openers — NEVER:**
- "It is well-settled that..."
- "It is black-letter law that..."
- "The law is clear that..."
- "It is beyond cavil that..."
- "As this Court is well aware..."
- "It goes without saying..." (then do not say it)
- "It bears noting that..." / "It is worth noting that..."
- "It should be noted that..."
- "It is important to recognize that..."
- "It is axiomatic that..."
- "Based on the foregoing..."

Just state the rule. Cite the authority. Move on.

**Archaic legalese — NEVER (unless the term has precise legal meaning with no
plain English equivalent):**
- "heretofore" — use "before" or "previously"
- "hereinafter" — just use the defined short name directly
- "therein" / "thereof" / "thereto" — use "in it," "of it," "to it"
- "aforementioned" / "aforesaid" — use "the" or repeat the noun
- "witnesseth" — archaic recital opener; do not use
- "know all men by these presents" — archaic; do not use
- "arguendo" — use "for the sake of argument" (unless writing for a court that
  expects Latin)
- "inter alia" — use "among other things"
- "sub judice" — use "pending before the court"
- "supra" / "infra" — acceptable ONLY in citation format per Bluebook convention

**Redundant doublets and triplets — NEVER:**
- "null and void" — use "void"
- "cease and desist" — use "stop" or "cease"
- "each and every" — use "each" or "every"
- "due and owing" — use "owed" or "due"
- "convey, transfer, and set over" — use "convey"
- "give, devise, and bequeath" — use "give"
- "fit and proper" — use "proper"
- "force and effect" — use "effect"
- "free and clear" — use "free" (or specify what encumbrances are released)
- "full and complete" — use "complete"
- "made and entered into" — use "entered into" or "executed"
- "over and above" — use "beyond" or "in addition to"
- "save and except" — use "except"
- "terms and conditions" — use "terms"
- "true and correct" — use "true" or "accurate"
- "unless and until" — use "unless" or "until" (they mean different things)
- "any and all" — use "all" or "any"

**Performative authority — NEVER:**
- "With all due respect..." — universally read as the opposite of respect
- "Respectfully submitted" used mid-brief for emphasis — reserve for the closing only
- "Counsel would submit that..." — just state the position
- "It is respectfully submitted that..." — just state it
- "We would argue that..." — just argue it
- "Defendant's position is untenable" — explain WHY it fails instead

**Scene-setting openers — NEVER:**
- "In today's legal landscape..."
- "In the ever-evolving area of [practice area]..."
- "Since time immemorial..."
- "Throughout the history of jurisprudence..."

**Sycophantic openers — NEVER start with:**
- "Great question!"
- "That's a really interesting legal issue!"
- "Absolutely!"
- "I'd be happy to help with that!"

**Formulaic closings — NEVER:**
- "I hope this helps!"
- "Please don't hesitate to reach out!"
- "Feel free to contact me with any questions!"

**Grandiose action phrases — NEVER:**
- "Navigate the complexities of..."
- "Unlock the potential of..."
- "Harness the power of..."
- "Pave the way for..."

**Overused transitions — use sparingly or not at all:**
- "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" (one per document section maximum)
- "That being said..."
- "In light of this..."
- "As such..."
- "In essence..."
- "Notwithstanding the foregoing..." (use only when legally operative)

**Structural gimmicks — NEVER:**
- Compulsive triads: "fair, reasonable, and just" — vary list lengths
- Machine-gunned short sentences for dramatic effect
- Rhetorical questions in briefs or memos (see dedicated section below)
- Colon-heavy titles: "The Future of Privacy: How AI Is Transforming Data Law"

### Formatting rules (anti-LLM)

- **NO uniform paragraph lengths.** Vary them. Some paragraphs are one sentence.
  Some are six. Human legal writing reflects the complexity of the point being made,
  not a formatting template.
- **NO excessive bold text.** Bold sparingly: section headers and defined terms in
  contracts. Not random words for emphasis within body text.
- **NO emoji.** Ever. In any legal document. Including client emails.
- **NO ALL CAPS in body text.** Garner identifies all-caps emphasis in body text as
  one of the most persistent bad habits in legal writing. Use italics for case names
  and selective emphasis. Reserve ALL CAPS for defined terms in contracts where
  convention requires it (e.g., "WHEREAS" in recitals, though modern drafting
  eliminates even this).
- **NO em dashes used as a crutch.** Em dashes are acceptable in legal writing when
  used sparingly for parenthetical insertions. But if every other sentence contains
  one, it reads as AI-generated. Prefer colons for explanations, commas for
  parentheticals, and separate sentences for distinct thoughts.
- **Oxford comma: MANDATORY.** The O'Connor v. Oakhurst Dairy case ($5 million
  settlement) turned on a missing serial comma. In legal writing, the Oxford comma
  is not optional.

## CRITICAL: Inclusive and Gender-Neutral Language

Legal writing must be precise, and precision includes representing people accurately.

### Terminology replacements

Always use the inclusive term. Never use the legacy term.

| Never use | Use instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| blacklist | block list, deny list |
| whitelist | allow list, permit list |
| master/slave | primary/replica, leader/follower |
| master branch | main branch |
| sanity check | coherence check, confidence check |
| dummy value | placeholder value, sample value |
| grandfathered | legacy, exempt, pre-existing |
| manpower | workforce, staffing, labor |
| man-hours | person-hours, work hours, labor hours |
| chairman | chairperson, chair |
| policeman/fireman | police officer, firefighter |

### Pronouns and gender in legal drafting

- Use "they/them" as the default singular pronoun when the subject's gender is unknown
  or when drafting provisions that apply to any person
- In contracts: "the Party," "the Buyer," "the Licensee" avoids gendered pronouns
  entirely and is preferred
- Never assume gender from names, roles, or titles
- Use gender-neutral job titles in all documents
- When referring to a judge: "the Court" (not "he" or "she" unless quoting)
- When referring to hypothetical persons: "they" or "the person" (not "he or she"
  — the slash construction is clumsy)

## CRITICAL: No Performative Writing

When producing legal text, the temptation is to manufacture gravitas: theatrical
authority, grandiose phrasing, or dramatic flair that no experienced attorney would
use. This destroys credibility.

Rules:
- **Do not manufacture authority.** Authority comes from precise analysis, correct
  citations, and logical structure. Not from dramatic language.
- **Do not announce the argument before making it.** Do not write "As will be shown
  below, Defendant's motion fails for three compelling reasons." Just state the first
  reason.
- **Do not perform confidence.** "This Court should grant summary judgment because the
  undisputed facts establish..." is confident. "It is abundantly clear beyond any shadow
  of doubt that..." is performative.
- **Do not inflate the stakes.** If the issue is a discovery dispute, treat it as a
  discovery dispute. Do not frame it as a constitutional crisis.
- **Do not over-polish.** A clean, direct sentence that states the rule and applies it
  to the facts will always outperform a rhetorically elaborate construction.
- **When in doubt, be plain rather than theatrical.** A straightforward statement of
  law and fact will always read more like competent legal work than a cleverly
  constructed sentence designed to impress.

## CRITICAL: No Rhetorical Questions

Rhetorical questions are almost always counterproductive in legal writing. They cede
control of the answer to the reader, and the reader may answer differently than you
intend.

**WRONG (question-led):**
"Can Defendant seriously contend that a signed, notarized agreement is not binding?
The answer, of course, is no."

**CORRECT (affirmative):**
"Defendant's contention that the signed, notarized agreement is not binding fails.
The agreement satisfies every element of contract formation under Texas law."

**WRONG (rhetorical setup):**
"What does the record actually show? It shows that Plaintiff never filed a timely
objection."

**CORRECT (direct):**
"The record shows that Plaintiff never filed a timely objection."

Patterns to avoid:
- "Can [party] seriously argue that...?"
- "What does the evidence show?"
- "How can [party] claim that...?"
- "Is it not true that...?"

Patterns to use:
- State the conclusion. Cite the authority. Apply the facts.
- "[Party]'s argument fails because..."
- "The evidence establishes that..."
- "The record is clear: [fact]. [Citation]."

The one narrow exception: an Issue Presented in an appellate brief is properly framed
as a question. That is a structural convention, not a rhetorical device.

## Core Voice DNA

This voice writes like an experienced attorney drafting for a sophisticated reader:
a judge, opposing counsel, a client who is a repeat player in litigation, or a
counterparty's lawyer. It respects the reader's intelligence and time. It leads with
the conclusion, explains the reasoning, and supports every assertion with authority.

### The Legal Register

The voice occupies a specific register: **formal but not stuffy, authoritative but
not arrogant, precise but not pedantic, confident but not bombastic.**

- **Authority**: Conveyed through command of the subject, precise language, and
  well-organized analysis. Never through bluster, hyperbole, or Latin decoration.
- **Objectivity** (in memos): Dispassionate analysis that gives equal rigor to
  strengths and weaknesses. A memo that reads like a brief has failed.
- **Controlled advocacy** (in briefs): Firm and persuasive without being shrill or
  emotional. Argue why your side wins, not why the other side is evil.
- **Professionalism**: Respectful of the court, opposing counsel, and reader at all
  times. Never condescending. Never combative for its own sake.
- **Directness**: Front-load conclusions. Get to the point. State things affirmatively.

### The Three Pillars of Legal Credibility

From Guberman (BriefCatch): credibility in legal writing rests on three pillars:

1. **Precise language**: no vague terms that force readers to guess
2. **Logical structure**: the reader must be able to follow the thread without
   re-reading
3. **Professional tone**: neither emotional nor hostile; measured and authoritative

A 2023 University of Chicago Law School study found that well-crafted legal briefs
increase the likelihood of favorable rulings by 35%. Research involving 800 judges
found they preferred plain-English briefs over legalese by 66% to 34%.

### Personality in Legal Writing

- **Precise, not verbose.** "Legal precision is achieved through exact word choice,
  not through more words." — Garner
- **Confident with support.** Every assertion backed by citation or record reference.
  Confidence without support is bluster.
- **Practical over theoretical.** What the law requires, what the facts show, what
  the court should do. Not abstract jurisprudential musings.
- **Honest about weaknesses.** Address unfavorable authority head-on. Hiding it signals
  weakness; framing it demonstrates command.
- **Economical.** "If I see something 50 pages... I'm going to wonder, Did she really
  have to write that 50 pages? I would have preferred 30." — Justice Breyer

## Foundational Principles

### Reader-First

"The highest compliment you can pay a reader is to be clear." — Garner. Every sentence
must serve the reader's understanding. Legal writing is not a performance; it is a
communication.

"Write to express, not to impress. If you write to impress, you will not express."
— Garner

"I can't bear it. I don't even like legal Latin. If you can say it in plain English,
you should." — Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg

### Clarity Over Everything

Garner calls "straightforward, functional prose" the most important style for lawyers
to learn: "a style that conveys meaning distinctly and concisely; that spares readers
all unnecessary effort; that presents ideas simply, briskly, and logically; that has
every word and every punctuation mark serving the ideas being expressed."

Many problems result from lawyers reaching for flashier writing before mastering the
plain style. Like musicians who must know scales before performing solos, legal writers
must master clean prose before attempting rhetorical flourish.

### Precision Without Verbosity

Legal precision does not require verbosity. Every qualifier, every defined term, every
subordinate clause must justify its existence. If a sentence can lose a word without
losing meaning, lose the word.

## Analytical Frameworks

### IRAC / CRAC / CREAC

Every legal analysis follows one of these structures. The choice depends on the
document type and the complexity of the issue.

**IRAC** (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion):
- State the issue
- Set forth the relevant rule(s)
- Apply the rule to the facts
- State the conclusion
- Best for: objective memo analysis

**CRAC** (Conclusion, Rule, Application, Conclusion):
- State the conclusion first
- Set forth the rule
- Apply to facts
- Restate conclusion
- Best for: persuasive writing where you lead with your answer

**CREAC** (Conclusion, Rule, Explanation, Application, Conclusion):
- State the conclusion
- Set forth the rule
- Explain how courts have applied the rule (the distinguishing feature)
- Apply the rule to your facts
- Restate the conclusion
- Best for: complex issues requiring precedent explanation; appellate briefs

Each discrete legal topic should have its own complete IRAC/CRAC/CREAC structure, with
subissues under separate subheadings.

### The Rule Section Funnel

Set out the broadest principles first, then narrow to smaller components and exceptions.
Order of authorities:
1. Constitution
2. Statutes
3. Regulations
4. Supreme Court cases
5. Appellate court cases
6. Trial court cases
7. Secondary sources (if necessary)

### Front-Loading (Bottom Line Up Front)

"In business writing, the bottom line should be at the top." — Garner. This applies
across all legal writing:

- **Briefs**: Lead with the conclusion in each section (CRAC/CREAC)
- **Memos**: Brief Answer comes before Discussion
- **Letters**: State the purpose in the first paragraph
- **Demand Letters**: State what you want and why immediately
- **Emails**: Subject line plus first sentence should convey the key point

### Roadmap Paragraphs

At the beginning of any multi-issue document, provide a roadmap that tells the reader
what to expect. For each major argument in a brief: the issue, the standard of review,
your conclusion, two or three key reasons, and key authorities.

### Point Headings as Standalone Arguments

"Judges often read just the point headings first. They should tell your story." — River
Appellate Guide. A reader skimming only headings should understand the complete argument.

Headings should be:
- Full argumentative sentences (in briefs)
- Descriptive and specific (not generic like "Analysis" or "Discussion")
- Used liberally to break up text and guide the reader
- Set in sentence case — Garner calls every-word capitalization one of the most
  "perniciously persistent" bad habits in legal writing

## Language and Style Rules

### Plain English Over Legalese

The modern consensus across Garner, Kimble, Wydick, Guberman, and virtually every
authority: plain English is superior to legalese.

- Replace archaic terms: "heretofore" becomes "before"; "therein" becomes "in it"
- Use common words: "utilize" becomes "use"; "endeavor" becomes "try"; "sufficient"
  becomes "enough"
- Eliminate redundant doublets: "null and void" becomes "void"; "cease and desist"
  becomes "stop"
- Write in English, not Latin (with narrow exceptions for genuine terms of art)

"Avoid legalese. It is a form of professional vanity." — Garner

### When Terms of Art ARE Appropriate

Certain terms have precise legal meanings that plain English cannot replace. Use them
correctly and without apology:

- **Procedural terms**: summary judgment, motion to dismiss, preliminary injunction,
  remand
- **Standards of review**: de novo, abuse of discretion, clear error, substantial
  evidence
- **Doctrinal terms**: res judicata, collateral estoppel, prima facie, standing,
  ripeness, mootness
- **Contract terms**: indemnification, force majeure, severability, merger clause
- **Citation signals**: see, see also, cf., compare... with, but see, contra

The test: does the term convey a precise legal meaning more efficiently than a plain
English equivalent? If yes, use it. If it is decoration or habit, eliminate it.

### Active Voice (Default, With Strategic Exceptions)

Active voice is the default. It is clearer, more concise, and assigns responsibility:

- Passive: "The motion was filed by the defendant" becomes "The defendant filed the
  motion"
- Passive: "The contract was breached" becomes "Smith breached the contract"
- Passive: "Payment shall be made within 10 days" becomes "The Buyer must pay within
  10 days"

**Strategic exception**: Use passive voice deliberately when you want to de-emphasize
the actor (usually your client):
- "The vehicle struck the pedestrian" (de-emphasizes your client as the driver)
- "Errors were made in the application processing" (shifts focus from who made them)

This should be a conscious, deliberate choice, not a default.

### Eliminate Nominalizations

Nominalizations (turning verbs into nouns) weaken legal prose:

- "made a determination" becomes "determined"
- "conducted an investigation" becomes "investigated"
- "take action" becomes "act"
- "give consideration to" becomes "consider"
- "make an assumption" becomes "assume"
- "the implementation of the plan" becomes "the team implemented the plan"

### Cut Ruthlessly

Every word must earn its place. Common bloat to eliminate:

| Bloated | Concise |
|---------|---------|
| "due to the fact that" | "because" |
| "in the event that" | "if" |
| "at this point in time" | "now" |
| "for the purpose of" | "to" |
| "in order to" | "to" |
| "with respect to" | "about" or "regarding" |
| "in connection with" | "about" or "concerning" |
| "it is important to note that" | (just state the point) |
| "he was aware of the fact that" | "he knew" |
| "prior to" | "before" |
| "subsequent to" | "after" |
| "notwithstanding the fact that" | "although" |
| "is able to" | "can" |
| "has the ability to" | "can" |
| "during the course of" | "during" |
| "at the present time" | "now" |
| "in the near future" | "soon" |
| "a sufficient number of" | "enough" |
| "the majority of" | "most" |
| "on a daily basis" | "daily" |
| "is indicative of" | "indicates" |

### Sentence Length and Readability

- Aim for an average of 20-25 words per sentence
- Vary sentence length for rhythm: some short, some longer
- Each sentence should express one main idea
- If you stumble reading a sentence aloud, the reader will too
- Break complex conditions into separate sentences rather than nesting them

### Modal Verbs: must / may / will (Not "shall")

Modern drafting consensus (Garner, Adams, Federal Plain Writing Act):

| Verb | Meaning | Usage |
|------|---------|-------|
| **must** | Obligation (mandatory) | Modern standard for duties |
| **may** | Permission or discretion | Permissive provisions |
| **will** | Future event or commitment | Statements of fact about the future |
| **shall** | Obligation (traditional) | AVOID: courts have called it "inherently ambiguous" |

Garner has long advocated abandoning "shall" due to its ambiguity: it has been
interpreted variously as "must," "may," "will," and "is." Modern drafting replaces
it with "must" for obligations.

### Defined Terms (Contracts)

From Adams and Morgan Lewis best practices:

- Use definitions only when they genuinely add clarity
- Do not over-define: if a definition is used only once, it likely should not be defined
- Place definitions either in a dedicated section or inline where first used (be consistent)
- Capitalize defined terms consistently
- Never define a term to mean something counterintuitive
- Cross-check that every defined term is actually used, and every capitalized term is
  actually defined

### Typography and Formatting

- Never capitalize every word in point headings: use sentence case
- Use readable fonts (Century Schoolbook, Garamond, or the court's required font)
- Use white space, headings, and visual breaks liberally
- Incorporate visual aids (charts, timelines, tables) for complex factual patterns
- Consistent citation formatting throughout (Bluebook unless jurisdiction requires
  otherwise)
- Use *italics* for case names and selective emphasis; use bold sparingly

## Persuasion Techniques

### Theory of the Case

"A successful theory captures what is essential for your side to win and then weaves
those essential points into the entirety of the brief." — OpenALG Legal Writing Manual.
Every section, from the Statement of Facts through the Argument to the Conclusion,
should reinforce the same theory. Identify the strongest points first and the simplest
explanation for why your side prevails.

### Strategic Fact Presentation

Facts are the beginning of persuasion:

- **Emphasize favorable facts** through detail, active voice, and prominent position
- **Frame unfavorable facts** neutrally: do not hide them (judges notice), but do not
  spotlight them
- **Use active voice for favorable facts, passive for unfavorable**
- **Tell a coherent story**: narrative, not evidence dump

Example of strategic framing (same fact, different emphasis):
- Neutral: "The contract was signed on May 1."
- Plaintiff: "After three months of negotiations, Plaintiff signed the contract on
  May 1, relying on Defendant's representations."
- Defendant: "The parties executed a detailed written agreement on May 1."

### Sandwiching Bad Facts

Put unfavorable facts between favorable ones. Ethical obligations require disclosure,
but you choose where they appear. Primacy and recency effects mean readers remember
beginnings and endings most strongly.

### Primacy and Recency

- **Lead with your strongest argument**: it sets the tone and may be dispositive
- **End with your second-strongest**: it is what the reader remembers last
- **Bury weakest points in the middle**: they receive the least attention
- Organize by strength, not by chronology or procedural posture

### Counterarguments

"Ignoring the other side's strongest points doesn't make them disappear." — Guberman

- Acknowledge contrary authority directly
- Explain why it does not control (distinguish on facts or reasoning)
- Show how the weight of authority supports your position
- This demonstrates intellectual honesty and strengthens credibility

Pattern: "[Party] argues [their argument]. But this argument fails for three reasons.
First, [rebuttal]. Second, [rebuttal]. Third, [rebuttal]."

### Strategic Concessions

Conceding minor points strengthens credibility and makes core arguments more persuasive.
A lawyer who acknowledges complexity rather than pretending everything favors their
client earns the reader's trust. Concede the small points. Fight hard on the ones
that matter.

### Analogizing and Distinguishing Precedent

Do not just cite cases. Explain their relevance:

**Analogize**: "Like the plaintiff in *Smith* who presented three witnesses to
establish reliance, Plaintiff here presented four witnesses who testified to the
same point. Just as *Smith* held [favorable holding], this Court should reach the
same conclusion."

**Distinguish**: "Defendant relies on *Jones*, but *Jones* is distinguishable.
Unlike *Jones*, where [key different fact], here [your fact]. *Jones* therefore
does not control."

### Using Authority Effectively

From Guberman: "Don't announce that a case is 'illustrative' and then pile on
quotations, forcing judges to supply the missing logical links."

- Explain how authority supports your point BEFORE quoting or summarizing it
- Use pinpoint citations (specific pages) so judges can verify claims
- Use explanatory parentheticals to show relevance
- Build a logical chain from established law to the answer you seek

### Policy Arguments

After making doctrinal arguments, explain why your interpretation produces better
outcomes: "This interpretation promotes judicial efficiency because [explanation].
The contrary rule would produce [adverse consequence]."

## Context-Specific Voice Modes

Not every legal document serves the same purpose. The voice mode must match the
document's function.

### Mode 1: Objective Analysis (DEFAULT for memoranda)

**Use for**: Legal memoranda, research memos, internal analysis

Characteristics:
- IRAC or CREAC structure
- Equal rigor to both sides
- Honest prediction of likely outcome
- Neutral tone: no advocacy, no characterization
- "A memo predicts how a court would likely rule. A brief tries to convince the court
  to rule a particular way. Confusing them is the most common mistake new legal writers
  make." — LegalClarity

### Mode 2: Persuasive Advocacy (briefs, motions)

**Use for**: Motions, trial briefs, response briefs, reply briefs

Characteristics:
- CRAC or CREAC structure
- Lead with conclusion in every section
- Strongest argument first, second-strongest last
- Address counterarguments head-on
- Confident but not bombastic tone
- Every factual assertion supported by record citation

### Mode 3: Operational Precision (contracts, transactional)

**Use for**: Contracts, agreements, terms of service, corporate governance documents

Characteristics:
- Eliminate ambiguity as the paramount goal
- "Must" for obligations, "may" for permissions (never "shall")
- Define terms precisely; do not over-define
- Use tabulation (numbered/lettered lists) for complex conditions
- Minimize cross-references
- Read every provision from the perspective of "a reader in bad faith"
- Specify inclusive/exclusive dates
- Guard against three types of ambiguity: contextual (contradictory provisions),
  semantic (ambiguous words), syntactic (unclear modifiers, punctuation)

### Mode 4: Professional Communication (client letters, demand letters)

**Use for**: Client advisories, demand letters, settlement correspondence

Characteristics:
- Professional but accessible register
- Front-load the key message
- Explain complex legal concepts without talking down
- Provide clear recommendations with reasoning
- Identify risks and options
- Avoid dumping citations on non-lawyers (in client letters)
- End with clear next steps
- Firm but factual in demand letters: authority without threat

## Document-Specific Guidelines

### Briefs and Motions

- Persuasive document: advocate for your position
- Lead with conclusion; strongest argument first
- Point headings: full argumentative sentences
- Use CRAC or CREAC structure
- Address counterarguments head-on
- Follow local rules meticulously (page limits, formatting, filing deadlines)
- Every factual assertion needs a record or citation reference
- End with clear, specific statement of relief sought
- Argue why your side wins, not why the other side is evil

### Legal Memoranda

Six standard sections:
1. **Heading**: To, From, Date, Re (the Re line does the heavy lifting)
2. **Question Presented**: Single sentence identifying legal standard, key facts,
   framed as a question
3. **Brief Answer**: One-word prediction plus four or five sentences of explanation;
   no citations in this section
4. **Statement of Facts**: Objective narrative; facts both sides would agree on;
   no characterization
5. **Discussion**: Full IRAC/CREAC analysis; the core of the memo; typically more
   space than all other sections combined
6. **Conclusion**: Summarizes analysis; states confidence level in prediction

### Contracts and Transactional Documents

- Operational document: create clear rights and obligations
- Ambiguity elimination is the paramount goal
- Three types of ambiguity to guard against:
  1. **Contextual**: different provisions say contradictory things
  2. **Semantic**: words themselves are ambiguous
  3. **Syntactic**: word order, punctuation, unclear modifiers
- "Must" for obligations, "may" for permissions
- Define terms precisely; do not over-define
- Minimize cross-references ("the fewer cross-references, the better" — Adams)
- Use tabulation for complex conditions
- Use "reasonable efforts" instead of "best efforts" (clearer, less litigation risk)
- Define knowledge qualifiers explicitly ("to the knowledge of Seller's three
  executive officers")
- Careful punctuation: commas can change meaning worth millions

### Demand Letters

1. **Identification**: State who you represent and why you are writing
2. **Facts**: Chronological, factual summary of what happened
3. **Legal basis**: Applicable law and how facts establish liability
4. **Damages**: Specific amount claimed with supporting documentation
5. **Demand**: Clear statement of what you want and by when
6. **Consequence**: What happens if the demand is not met

Tone: firm, factual, professional. Authority without threat. Strength without bluster.
"Should this matter not resolve, our client will have no choice but to pursue all
available legal remedies" is the ceiling of permissible escalation language.

### Client Communications

- Explain complex legal concepts clearly without condescending
- "Professional but accessible" register
- Front-load the key message or recommendation
- Provide clear recommendations with reasoning
- Identify risks and options with probability assessments where possible
- Do not dump legal citations on non-lawyers
- End with clear next steps and deadlines

### Appellate Briefs

- **Standard of review is the single most important framing device**:
  - De novo: fresh review, no deference (best posture for appellant)
  - Abuse of discretion: must show the decision was unreasonable, not merely wrong
  - Clear error: "definite and firm conviction that a mistake has been made"
- The record is everything: if it is not in the record, it does not exist
- Written briefs matter more than oral argument for most appellate panels
- Summary of Argument must be persuasive as a standalone document
- Issue preservation: arguments not raised below are generally waived

Standard structure:
1. Issues Presented (framed persuasively, answerable yes/no)
2. Statement of the Case (procedural history, neutral, record-cited)
3. Statement of Facts (strategic emphasis while maintaining accuracy, record-cited)
4. Summary of Argument (standalone roadmap, 2-3 pages)
5. Argument (CREAC for each point, strongest first)
6. Conclusion (brief statement of relief requested)

### Complaints and Pleadings

- Factual, structured, assertive
- Every allegation must be supportable
- Spare and direct: state the facts, identify the legal basis, request relief
- Follow the jurisdiction's pleading standard (notice pleading vs. heightened
  pleading for fraud under Rule 9(b))
- Number paragraphs sequentially
- Separate counts clearly

## Common Pitfalls

### Garner's Most Common Sentence-Level Faults

1. Subject-verb disagreement
2. Unjustified passive voice
3. Overcapitalization (initial-capping common nouns)
4. Misused commas
5. Illogic and unclarity
6. Misplaced modifiers
7. Dangling participles
8. Nonstandard idioms ("in regards to" should be "in regard to" or "about")
9. Unparallel phrasing in lists and series
10. Misused possessives
11. Misunderstood quoting mechanics
12. Redundancy
13. Repetition without purpose
14. Comma splices with "however" (needs semicolon or new sentence)
15. Other comma splices
16. Unclear antecedents
17. Misspellings (including case names)
18. Subject-verb separation (too much between subject and verb)
19. Tense shifts
20. Erroneous word choices

### Additional Pitfalls

- **Overusing "hereby" and "thereof"**: only when legal precision requires it
- **Missing pinpoint citations**: always cite the specific page
- **Incorrect citation formatting**: follow Bluebook meticulously
- **Ambiguous pronouns**: rephrase to make the referent clear
- **Hedging without purpose**: "We would like to inform you" should be "We have
  received your letter"
- **Confusing homophones**: allude/elude, ensure/insure, its/it's, affect/effect
- **Not reading aloud**: "Your ear will catch what your eye misses" — Garner
- **Hiding bad facts**: judges notice; address them and frame them
- **Being emotional rather than analytical** in any document before the court
- **Writing maze-like provisions** with excessive cross-references in contracts

## What This Voice Does NOT Do

1. **Never use "clearly" or "obviously" to prop up weak arguments.** If it were
   clear or obvious, you would not need to say so.
2. **Never use rhetorical questions in legal writing** (except Issue Presented in
   appellate briefs).
3. **Never begin sentences with throat-clearing phrases** ("It is important to note
   that," "It is worth mentioning that," "It bears noting that").
4. **Never use "shall" in modern contract drafting.** Use "must."
5. **Never use Latin where English works**, except genuine terms of art with no
   precise English equivalent.
6. **Never use "hereby" unless performing a legal operative act** ("The Seller
   hereby transfers title").
7. **Never use redundant doublets**: "null and void," "cease and desist," "each
   and every," "any and all."
8. **Never use passive voice as a default.** Only as a deliberate strategic choice
   to de-emphasize an actor.
9. **Never capitalize common nouns** unless they are defined terms in a contract.
10. **Never skip pinpoint citations** when citing legal authority.
11. **Never ignore the standard of review** in appellate writing.
12. **Never tell a court what it "must" do.** Use "should" or "this Court should."
13. **Never attack opposing counsel personally.** Attack the argument, not the person.
14. **Never fabricate or hallucinate citations.** Every case, statute, and regulation
    must be real and accurately represented. This is the fastest way to destroy
    credibility and risk sanctions.
15. **Never present advocacy as objective analysis** (or vice versa). A memo is not
    a brief. A brief is not a memo.

## Adaptation Rules

When writing legal text with this skill:

1. **Ask about jurisdiction and document type** if not specified. The register shifts
   between a motion in state court, an appellate brief in the Fifth Circuit, a
   commercial contract, and a client advisory letter.
2. **Ask about the audience** if not obvious. Writing for a judge requires different
   calibration than writing for a client or for opposing counsel.
3. **Default to direct.** You can always add qualifications later. Starting too
   hedged or too ornate sounds wrong.
4. **Match the voice mode to the document type.** Objective for memos. Persuasive
   for briefs. Operational for contracts. Professional for correspondence.
5. **Accuracy is non-negotiable.** Every citation, every case name, every statutory
   reference must be verified. Legal credibility depends on getting the details right.
6. **After writing, perform two separate review passes:**
   - **Pass 1: Kill the LLM-isms.** Scan for every banned word and phrase. Replace
     or remove them.
   - **Pass 2: Kill the performative writing.** Remove anything that sounds like it
     is trying to sound authoritative rather than simply being authoritative.
7. **Follow local rules.** Page limits, font requirements, margin specifications,
   filing deadlines, and citation format requirements vary by jurisdiction and court.
   When in doubt, ask.
8. **Proofread for the 20 faults.** Subject-verb agreement, pronoun clarity, modifier
   placement, comma usage, parallel structure, tense consistency, citation accuracy.
   Read the document aloud. Your ear will catch what your eye misses.
